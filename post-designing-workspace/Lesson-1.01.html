Lesson 1.01 at BBDC

By: Mallvin Rajamohan

Posted on: 2020-09-22

A recap on my first motorcycle lesson at BBDC and how it went. I'm sure there are useful bits of information that can help you.

I have decided to post a recap on all my lessons for my class 2B which i passed a couple of weeks ago. It was hard but i really enjoyed riding. I have a bike now (Bajaj Pulsar NS200) and i will be making posts on it too. I hope you find all these posts useful while you learn how to ride.

Introduction

It was finally my first practical lesson. I didn't know what to expect going into it. If you are not from Singapore, the way the motorcycle license system works is that there are 3 classes. Class 2B permits you to ride motorcycle not exceeding 200CC in engine capacity. Class 2A is between 200 and 400CC. Class 2 is anything above 400CC which where the good stuff is at. I like this system as it deters people from taking a big bike as their first bike.

I was worried mainly about balancing and handling the bike. When the lesson started, we were told to put on our elbow and knee guards (a requirement for lessons 1.01 to 1.03). We were then briefed what we were going to be taught. In front of me were rows of CB190Rs. The bikes looked huge. With the added width from the crash bars (which i learnt the hard way are meant to protect the bike and not me).

Controls of the motorcycle

We were introduced to the controls of the motorcycle. There are 2 types of controls, primary and secondary. Primary controls are used to operate the bike, such as the throttle, front and rear brakes, gear shift lever and clutch. Secondary controls are like the signal indicators, horn and headlights. I shall seperate it into 2 lists so that it is easier to comprehend.

Primary Controls
  • Throttle
  • Clutch
  • Front Brake
  • Rear Brake
  • Gear shift lever
Secondary Controls
  • Signal Indicators
  • Horn
  • Headlight

Before each lesson and before we rode the bike, we had to do a pre ride check to make sure the bike is working and nothing is broken. First, we turn the key and start the bike. We tap the horn button to make sure the horn is working, the we flip the indicator switches to the left and right to check them. Look at the front indicators then the rear. Both should be working. Then we press the front brake lever and look at the light on the front on the bike (the schools attach a row of 3 lights that are linked to the front brake, rear brake and the last light turns on if the speed of the bike is over 30km/h). Then cup the rear brake light and see if it is on. Do the same thing for the rear brake. Also, don't forget to look at the display to see if there is enough fuel in the bike. Once done, switch the bike off.

Pushing and Parking the bike

The Cb190R was a pretty big bike and it was heavy as hell. We had to put it on its main stand which parks the bike upright on 2 'feet'. To do so, we put our left hand on the left handle bar and right hand on the rear rear or crash bar and put one foot on top of the main stand leg, then push down on it with the help of our bodyweight and at the same time pulling it up with our right hand at the crash bar. Make sure both legs are on the ground on the bike will tip over. To take it off the main stand, we placed the right leg next to the bike and the left leg slighly in front of the right leg for balance. Then you cover the front brake, don't press it or the bike won't come down, and rock the bike back and forth till it shifts forward and lands on the wheel. Then gently apply the front brake. Don't grab or slam the brake, it will fall over. Also keep the handle bars straight while braking. You also want to keep the bike upright and close to you.

Mounting and Dismounting

For mounting, you first have to take the bike off the main stand and hold the front brake. Then, you check back on your right to see if anyone or vehicle is coming before you swing your leg over. When you swing your leg over the bike, it is advised to get your right foot onto the rear brake and press it. Then you sit onto the bike. For dismounting, you check back right again, then while holding the front brake, swing your leg back over. Don't rush or you might drop the bike.

Riding Posture

We were thought 7 points of correct riding posture. But first lets quickly go over the benefits of adopting the proper riding posture.

  • Greater riding comfort
  • Improve control of the bike - making it easy to reach for the controls
  • Increase stability of the bike
  • Better awareness of changing road and traffic conditions ahead and around you

Eyes

Lets go over each posture one by one. First, we shall start with the eyes. The most commonly thought idea is look where you want to go. It is fundamental for riding a bike. Even when walking, you always look where you want to go. Try looking down at the floor and walking or even turning. It will be hard and you might even fall or lose balance. Same goes for a motorcycle. So chin up and look far ahead.

Shoulders

You want to keep your shoulders relaxed and depressed. Not elevated as it makes it harder to control the bike and makes your entire body feel tight and tense, causing you to make jerky and hard inputs.

Elbows

Bend your elbows slighly and keep them relaxed.

Hands

Grip the handles gently, not as hard as you can with a death grip.

Buttocks

Keep your butt as close to the tank as possible so that you have a comfortable reach of the handlebars.

Knees

Grip the fuel tank with your knees for stability. Most of the maneuvering of a motorcycle comes from your hips.

Feet

Rest the arches of your feet on the pegs and keep your toes pointed straight so you have good control of the pedals. Cover both pedals with your feet. Don't tuck them under them.This will help lower your reaction time.

Clutch, Throttle and Brake control

By far the most important part of this lesson. The 3 main primary controls you use to control the bike.

Clutch

The clutch temporarily disconnects the engine from the transmission, which means no drive is going into the rear wheel. The picture above shows the man holding it with 2 fingers, but for beginners, it is advised that you hold it with 4 fingers as you may not have the strength in your fingers to operate the clutch with 2 fingers yet. At lower speeds and gears, the clutch has to be released with a lot of control. Too fast and the bike will stall. At slow speeds, you use the clutch to control your speed not the throttle. Just maintain the throttle while releasing and pulling the clutch with very slighly movements (we are talking milimeters) to control the speed of the bike. We first practised moving with just the clutch and no throttle. Before that, lets go over the biting point/frinction zone. The biting point is the point where the engine and transmission begin to slighly touch. They are 100% touching each other yet, maybe like 2-5%. What you want to do is gradually allow the engine to transmit force to the rear wheel. If you do it too fast(dumping the clutch), the engine won't have enough force to get the rear wheel moving. Give the transmission time to match rotation of the rear wheel. You want to gradually transmit the force of the engine to the rear wheel. If you release the clutch too fast or dump it, the whole power is being transmitted suddenly resulting in a jerky start and a probable stall of the engine. This gradual loading allows the bike engine to transfer the necessary torque to the stationary wheel.

Throttle

For the throttle, what you want to do is hold it gently. I learnt from first hand experience that if you hold on to it tightly with a death grip, you will make big and jerky inputs. However, if you hold it gently, you can make minor small inputs and your ride won't be so jerky.

Brakes

For the front brakes, when you want to brake, you gradually apply pressure starting with a light touch. Don't grab with with all your strength. The front wheel won't have enough traction as all the weight is on the rear wheel and the front wheel will end up skidding and you will fall. Grab with lightly with like 5-10% force and slowly increase pressure over time (20%, 30%, 40% etc). The rear brake is not used as much as the front. The ratio in which you use the brake is 70% front and 30% rear. I am not saying don't use your rear brake, but instead, use more of your front as compared to the rear. Same thing applies to the rear brake, gradually apply force. This is called progressive brake pressure. At slower speeds, when you are about to stop, using more rear brake will help you avoid hard braking which happens when you use too much front brake and the front suspension compresses down hard.
